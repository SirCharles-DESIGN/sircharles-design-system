# SirCharles.DESIGN — Voice & Tone

How SirCharles.DESIGN sounds, derived from the actual copy on sircharles.design (April 2026). Every voice attribute below is supported by a verbatim example pulled from the live site.

## Voice in one paragraph

SirCharles speaks like a senior craftsperson who has built ambitious things and would rather show you the result than describe the process. The tone is **confident without being boastful** — claims always come with a number or a recognizable client. Metaphors are precise and athletic (chess, supercars, satellites), never decorative. Sentences are tight, often two or three words. Outcomes lead; methodology follows.

Distinctive moves: pairing a poetic statement with a concrete claim ("Methodology meets artistry. Award-winning design."), grounding abstract value in measurable shifts ("25× efficiency," "1 star to 4.7 stars," "$65M acquisition"), and addressing the prospective client as a peer who already understands quality.

## We Are / We Are Not

The core identity anchor. When in doubt, hold the proposed copy against this table.

| ✅ We Are | ❌ We Are Not |
|---|---|
| **Confident** — claims sit beside evidence ("$65M acquisition," "25× boost") | Boastful — vague superlatives, "best in class," "world-leading" without proof |
| **Precise** — short sentences, exact numbers, named clients (Hypergiant, 7-Eleven, Oracle, NASA) | Hedgy — "leveraging," "robust solutions," "best-in-breed" jargon |
| **Crafted** — metaphors are exact and earned ("supercar standard," chess phases, satellite ops) | Cute — wordplay, puns, emoji-driven copy |
| **Strategic** — frames work as business outcomes ("transformed declining product into modern competitor") | Tool-talk — "we use Figma and React" feature lists |
| **Direct** — verbs do the work ("rescued," "rebuilt," "tackled") | Passive — "solutions were delivered," "engagements were undertaken" |
| **Premium-informal** — first-person, contractions, "Let's talk" | Stiff — corporate-speak, third-person bio style |
| **Visually literate** — comfortable describing aesthetics in concrete terms | Visually vague — "modern," "clean," "user-friendly" left undefined |

## Voice constants vs. tone flexes

**Voice** stays the same across every surface. **Tone** flexes by context.

### Voice constants (always)

- One typeface in writing too: **clear, declarative English**
- Outcomes before methodology
- Proof close to claim (a number, a client name, an award, within the same sentence or the next one)
- First-person singular when speaking as Charles ("I"), first-person plural ("we") when speaking as the practice
- Title case for proper product names (HIVE, Skipcart, Cub3d) and ALL CAPS only for product packs (NIMIS 350, AV OPTICS) — never for emphasis

### Tone flexes (by context)

| Context | Formality | Energy | Length | Example tone |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hero / homepage | Medium | High | Very short | "Methodology meets artistry. Award-winning design." |
| Case study lead | Medium | Medium | Medium | "Changed how satellite operations work by creating software that lets single operators manage 75–100 spacecraft at once." |
| About / personal bio | Low | Reflective | Long | "To solve complex problems, I go beyond surface-level analysis by diving deep into a client's business world." |
| Contact / outreach | Low | Warm | Short | "Thank you for visiting my portfolio. I'm excited to share my work and explore new opportunities…" |
| Client testimonial | Quoted verbatim | — | — | "Skipcart got the application it always wanted. It was visually pleasing—clean, and easy to use." |
| Award / press blurb | Medium | High | Short | "2024 Netty Award for Best User Interface Integration" |
| Product / decal pack | High (technical) | Medium | Medium | "NIMIS 350 Professional Sci-Fi Design Systems — DECALmachine & KIT OPS Ready" |
| Social caption | Low | High | Very short | "Say Hello." |

## Signature moves

Distinctive constructions to reach for first. These appear across the live site and define the brand's literary fingerprint.

### 1. The two-beat headline

A poetic claim followed by a concrete one. Reads like a couplet.

> Methodology meets artistry.
> Award-winning design.

> Complex ideas. Simple products. Real results.

### 2. The metric-anchored sentence

State the change in one sentence; lead with the number.

> 25× operational efficiency gains — one person can now manage what used to take 25 people.
> App rating jumped from 1 star to 4.7 stars.
> $65M acquisition by 7-Eleven in 2022.

### 3. The recognizable-client name-drop (no embellishment)

Just say the name. Don't decorate.

> H-E-B's pharmacy app had a devastating 1-star rating…
> Bo Jackson became one of the first celebrities authenticating memorabilia through Genuine.

### 4. The earned metaphor

Metaphors are sustained — chess for methodology, supercars for craft standard, satellites for the actual work.

> Three phases: Opening Strategy, Fluid Middlegame, Precise Endgame.
> Distinguishing a Toyota Camry from a Koenigsegg Jesko.

### 5. The action verb that does work

Choose verbs that imply heavy lifting: *rescued, rebuilt, tackled, transformed, eliminated, expanded, attracted*.

> Rescued Inmar's sophisticated but unused filtering system…
> Tackled fraud in sports and entertainment memorabilia…

## Don't write like this

The following are real-world failure modes for this voice. Reject any draft that drifts here.

| ❌ Don't | Why it's off-brand |
|---|---|
| "We are a passionate team of innovative thinkers leveraging best-in-class methodologies." | Hedgy, jargon-heavy, no proof, plural where singular belongs. |
| "Charles is a designer who designs designs." | Too cute. The brand never plays this card. |
| "Cutting-edge, next-gen, AI-powered design solutions for tomorrow's enterprise." | Stacked buzzwords with no claim behind them. |
| "Our process involves the following 12 steps…" | Process before outcome. The voice leads with what changed, not how. |
| "Drop us a line! 🚀✨" | Emoji + exclamation stack. The voice is warm but never effusive. |
